The South Quay has a lighthouse now, and a proper one — thirty blocks of coursed grey stone rising from the point beyond the harbour wall, a lamp room glazed clean round, and a beam that sweeps the width of the Vale Channel’s mouth twice to the minute. It took its first watch this week, on a night thick with the kind of fog that has always been the quay’s particular trouble. Nobody lost the light.

Article IV names lighthouses among the common works, alongside roads, bridges and walls, and this one was raised the same way the Great North Road was: by charter, by open subscription of labour, and rather more slowly than anyone promised at the outset. The masons’ guild cut the tower from North Range stone, quarried from the same lots that supplied the National Monument’s restoration, and the lens and lamp glass were ground and blown in the South Quay workshops, over a winter of trial panes that did not, by the workshop’s own account, all survive the trying.

::: callout (decree) — Proclamation of the Chancellery
By decree of the Chancellery, the South Quay Lighthouse is entered among the common works of the nation, held in trust for every vessel that must find the channel after dark. A Keeper of the Light is appointed to its tending, on the same footing as the Keeper of Records — and charged, in Article V’s own words, that the light shall not be allowed to fail.
:::

The Keeper of Records is on record as finding the repetition satisfying rather than lazy — Article V, the Keeper points out, was written in words general enough to mean whatever the nation next needed them to mean, and raising an actual lighthouse before the nation’s first anniversary is exactly the sort of coincidence a founding document ought to be given credit for. The first Keeper of the Light was sworn in at the tower door at dusk, lit the lamp from a taper carried up from the Monument’s own flame, and by all reports got the wick right on the second attempt.

::: quote — The Keeper of the Light, at the tower’s first lighting
You learn a channel by its bad nights, not its good ones. We have had lanterns, bonfires, and one memorable brazier that blew over in a gale and very nearly took the pilot house with it. What we did not have, until this week, was a light that did not need someone to mind it hand and foot. I intend to mind it anyway. Old habits.
:::

The tower stands a full eight blocks taller than first charted. The original plan called for two-and-twenty blocks, on the reasoning that the point itself sits high enough; the Road Commission — lent to the project for its scaffolding, and evidently unable to resist an opinion — argued for the full thirty, on the grounds that a light seen late is a light that has already failed once. The Chancellery settled the matter by agreeing with the Commission and crediting the extra stone to “settling requirements.” Nothing has settled. The tower is thirty blocks because thirty blocks sees further, and everyone concerned is content to leave it there.

The Keeper of the Light stands night watch through the season, relieved by apprentices from the South Quay workshops on a rota the Chancellery will post alongside the harbour notices. Boats are asked, as ever, to give the point a wide berth after dark until they have learned the light’s rhythm; the harbour-master’s office will have the timing posted at the quay steps this week. The Road Commission’s next common work is the South Road, for which the Chancellery has been taking proposals since the North Road opened — the harbour wall repairs, residents are told, remain queued behind it.
